Geo-Insight

Which humanitarian crises are most overlooked?

When nearly every response is underfunded, a single coverage ratio no longer discriminates. Geo-Insight treats overlookedness as a latent scalar θ and infers its posterior from six signals through a hierarchical Bayesian model, calibrated against NUTS on every fit and validated externally against CERF Underfunded Emergencies and CARE Breaking the Silence.

§ 3 · The model

One latent. Three likelihoods. A calibrated posterior.

Overlookedness is a latent scalar $\theta(u)$, inferred from six attribute likelihoods matched to each attribute's support. Sign-constrained positive slopes make larger attribute values mean more overlooked.

Beta regression $a_i \mid \theta \sim \mathrm{Beta}\bigl(\sigma(\alpha_i + \beta_i\theta)\,\phi_i,\;(1-\sigma(\alpha_i + \beta_i\theta))\,\phi_i\bigr)$
Log-normal $\log a_2 \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_2 + \beta_2\theta,\,\sigma_2^2)$
Ordered logistic $a_4 \mid \theta \sim \mathrm{OrderedLogistic}(\alpha_4 + \beta_4\theta,\,\kappa)$

A Gaussian population prior on $\theta$ pools partial-data countries: sparser data, wider posteriors, by construction. Inference is variational with a full-covariance Gaussian guide (3 s on CPU), calibrated against NUTS on every fit.

§ 4 · What the posterior says

Twenty-two crises on one axis.

Every HRP-eligible country on one latent axis, with a 90 % credible interval. The top ten, highlighted, are the crises the model places at the overlooked end of the active-response pool.

Ordering over absolute values. The scale is the posterior median of $\theta$, recentered on the median country. Right is more overlooked; raw numbers have no external anchor. Only ordering and interval overlap carry meaning.

Set membership over ranking. The 90 % intervals overlap heavily across the top ten — within-top-10 ordering is not supported by the data. The defensible claim is membership: these ten crises, with comparable evidence behind each.

Funding-overlookedness axis. Coverage shortfall ($r = 0.76$) and donor concentration ($r = 0.82$) drive the latent. Need, sectoral equity, and severity sit near attribute ceilings across the HRP pool — they constrain the posterior without differentiating between countries.

Seven of the ten countries highlighted here also appear on CERF Underfunded Emergencies allocations in 2024–25 — the model recovers expert consensus with a credible interval on every pick.
§ 5 · Stakeholders, consensus, contested

Four lenses, one dataset.

Stakeholder disagreement is a difference between priors over the six slopes $\beta$, not weights in a sum. We pre-register four lenses: CERF (severity), ECHO (sectoral equity), USAID (donor concentration), NGO consortia (cluster equity). Each produces a separate posterior on $\theta$.

Honduras — most contested. Three lenses cluster near the same modestly-overlooked position; USAID's density sits visibly right. All four keep HND in the top-10 — the degree of overlookedness depends on whose priors you apply.

Haiti — most consensus. All four densities stack almost perfectly. The data overrides every prior; a safe pick under any lens.

Across the pool: 5 of 22 are consensus, 11 contested under at least one lens. Contestation is itself the signal — it flags where stakeholder dialogue matters most for allocation.

Pool: 22 HRP-eligible countries — those with a formal HNO filed and positive FTS requirements, the same population CERF UFE largely draws from. Eight CERF UFE / CARE BTS picks (AGO, BDI, ETH, MDG, MRT, MWI, SYR, ZMB) sit outside this pool because they lack the HRP data infrastructure. Expanding the gate either reintroduces the non-HRP pathology (small flash appeals whose coverage-shortfall attribute saturates at 1) or degrades precision @ k. Held-out test: a 2024-only fit predicts March-2025 CERF picks at 4/10 without seeing any 2025 data.

§ 7 · Forecasting monthly severity

Persistence is the wall. At 12 months we clear it.

Up to here the model is cross-sectional — a snapshot. Over the 65-month INFORM panel we can also forecast. A 2-block selective state-space model (Mamba, 19k parameters) reads 24 months per country and predicts the mean severity $H$ months out on the 1–5 scale. No funding data, no stakeholder priors, no Bayesian θ — just the monthly panel. Temporal split at January 2025.

The headline at 12 months. Mamba MAE 0.294 · persistence 0.407 (1.38× better) · seasonal 0.402 (1.37×) · mean 0.928 (3.15×). Not a big model — 19k parameters, two selective-SSM blocks, two minutes on a CPU — but the horizon is where trajectory information compounds and a static ``last value'' rule runs out. Short horizons remain persistence's territory; we don't overclaim.

As an L4 feature in the Bayesian LVM. The per-country forecast is registered in the ontology as severity_momentum_learned and fed to the LVM as a seventh sign-constrained attribute. On the 22-country HRP pool this currently moves the top-10 by three countries without a net precision gain across the four external benchmarks — honest extension, no uplift to claim. The forecasting result above is the standalone contribution.
