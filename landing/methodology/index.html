Methodology

From observation to overlookedness

A latent-variable model for ranking humanitarian crises under incompleteness, temporal drift, and multi-stakeholder ambiguity.

Also available as a paper: proposal.pdf

§ 1The problem

Sorting humanitarian crises by funding coverage used to work. It no longer does, because almost every active response is now in the failure zone together.

The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks $\$33$ billion to reach 135 million people. The 2025 appeal raised $\$12$ billion — the lowest in a decade. Nineteen country contexts sit below fifty per cent coverage, up from eight in 2021.

A single coverage ratio places all of them in a dense band near the floor. It doesn't distinguish between the three faces a neglected crisis can have: forgotten (unfunded and unnoticed — Angola 2023), contested (unfunded despite global attention — Sudan 2025), or sector-starved (adequate in aggregate while one sector sits at twenty per cent). Three problems, three different responses. The rest of this document describes a model that separates them.

§ 2A quantity we cannot see

The thing we want to measure — how overlooked a crisis is — is not written down anywhere. We have to infer it.

Call it $\theta(u, t)$: one real number per country $u$ per month $t$. It is a fiction — no one has ever written it down — but a useful one, because it lets us ask a sharper question than which crisis has the lowest coverage ratio. The sharper question is: given the evidence we do have, what does the data say about $\theta$, and how confident should we be?

The reframe
This turns the problem from scoring into inference. Our output is not a number — it is a posterior distribution over $\theta$ per country, whose width honestly reports what the evidence can and cannot tell us. A single number, however cleverly aggregated, cannot carry that uncertainty.

§ 3The clues we see

Six clues. Four different shapes. Each gets an equation matched to what it actually is.

We work with six: funding coverage shortfall, unmet amount per person in need, fraction of the population in need, INFORM severity category, donor concentration, and how evenly funding is split across sectors. Three live on $[0, 1]$. One is a positive real spanning orders of magnitude. One is an ordinal $\{1, \ldots, 5\}$. Any of them can be missing.

For the bounded fractions, beta regression keeps the predicted mean on $[0, 1]$: $a_i \mid \theta \sim \mathrm{Beta}\bigl(\mu_i\phi_i,\,(1-\mu_i)\phi_i\bigr)$ with $\mu_i = \sigma(\alpha_i + \beta_i\theta)$. For the per-person gap, $\log a_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_2 + \beta_2\theta,\,\sigma_2^2)$. For severity, ordered probit with four cut-points $\kappa_1 < \kappa_2 < \kappa_3 < \kappa_4$ we infer alongside everything else — we let the data say how INFORM actually spaces its labels.

For missing observations, nothing. No imputation. The likelihood contribution is simply zero. This is not a convention — it falls out of conditional probability. And it has a consequence the whole system depends on: a country with sparse data produces a wider posterior, never a confidently extreme one. Fig 2(d) shows exactly this.

§ 4Crises have memory

Sudan in April 2025 is not independent of Sudan in March 2025. The model needs to know this.

A country's overlookedness has a baseline and has episodes. The simplest honest way to say so is an AR(1) with country-specific parameters:

$$ \theta(u, t) \;=\; \mu_u \;+\; \rho_u\bigl(\theta(u, t-1) - \mu_u\bigr) \;+\; \eta_u(t), \qquad \eta_u(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \tau_u^2). $$

Three quantities fall out. $\mu_u$ is the country's long-run baseline — the chronic component. The deviation $\theta(u, t) - \mu_u$ is the acute component — how this month compares to the country's usual level. $\rho_u$ says how long shocks persist. None is computed separately; all three come from the same posterior.

§ 5Stakeholders, as priors

Instead of asking what weight severity should have in a formula, we ask what each stakeholder believes about severity, a priori. The difference sounds subtle. It isn't.

CERF emphasises severity and coverage shortfall. ECHO leans on equity. USAID looks at donor concentration. NGO consortia weight sector-equity. In the old machinery, that was a disagreement about numbers in a weighted sum. In our framing, each stakeholder $s$ is a prior over the six attribute slopes:

$$ \beta_i^{(s)} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(m_i^{(s)},\; v_i^{(s)}\bigr), \qquad i = 1, \ldots, 6. $$

The mean $m_i^{(s)}$ says what the stakeholder believes a priori; the variance $v_i^{(s)}$ says how tightly. Each stakeholder produces a separate posterior over $\theta$.

Three things follow. First, the priors are falsifiable: after fitting, the posterior on $\beta$ either corroborates a stakeholder's belief or moves away from it. If CERF's prior centres the coverage slope at 1.5 but the data pull the posterior to 0.8, we have learned something concrete about CERF's stated-versus-effective preference — a finding no weighted sum can produce. Second, stakeholder disagreement is no longer a four-point spread; it is the overlap of four posterior distributions, which we can measure directly. Third, the method is honestly context-sensitive: where data is abundant, all four posteriors converge; where it is sparse, stakeholder differences show up more. The system adapts to how much evidence it has.

§ 6What comes out

Every rank comes with its uncertainty, and every rank can be decomposed into the signals that produced it.

For each country, month, and stakeholder, we take posterior samples and read off: a posterior median rank, a 90 % credible interval on that rank, an attribute-level contribution decomposition showing which signals drove it, and a stakeholder-aggregated consensus/contested score.

With four stakeholder posteriors per country, we can measure how much they overlap. A country is consensus when all four distributions concentrate on the same range; it is contested when they separate. The consensus score is the fraction of posterior samples in which the country is in the top-$K$ under all four stakeholders simultaneously. The contested score is the spread of posterior medians across stakeholders. Fig 5 shows one country of each kind.

§ 7Validation, and what we are not claiming

A model's job is not to feel right. It is to agree with reality, or fail noisily.

We validate in three pre-registered ways. Temporal held-out: fit on data through December 2024, predict 2025 posterior ranks, compare against OCHA's CERF Underfunded Emergencies selections for 2025; report top-$K$ overlap and rank correlation with credible intervals. Attribute masking: take a fully-observed country, mask each attribute in turn, confirm that the credible interval on the rank widens predictably. Consistency: under a diffuse stakeholder prior the posterior median ranking should reproduce the weighted-sum ranking we shipped in the first version of the system.

What the system does is produce ranked posteriors, traceable to source data, with declared uncertainty. What it does not do is equally important. It does not forecast — the next layer handles that. It does not make causal claims; the observation model is association, not intervention. It does not replace judgement; it produces a ranked list, a coordinator decides. The benchmarks we validate against — CERF UFE, CARE's Breaking the Silence — are themselves imperfect proxies, reflecting political process and media attention respectively. The stakeholder priors are judgement, not calibration; regressing actual selections on the attributes to recover a stakeholder's effective prior is a natural extension we have not done yet. And fundamentally, $\theta$ is never directly observed — every posterior statement is contingent on the observation model being approximately correct. Validation gives us evidence. It does not give us proof.

§ 8The learned layer

The six signals above are themselves aggregates. The information they throw away is what the next layer will carry.

Under our six aggregates sits a richer raw panel: eighteen INFORM sub-indicators on displacement, casualties, access, and phase-level need; monthly flows donor by donor; sector-level breakdowns. A sequence model trained on that panel — Mamba, a masked-attention encoder — can compress the raw history into an embedding $z(u, t)$ that carries what the aggregates cannot. The extension slots in cleanly: replace $a_i \mid \theta$ with $\{a, z\} \mid \theta$. The latent $\theta$ stays scalar and interpretable. The encoder has a crisp objective — reduce posterior variance on $\theta$ given the evidence — and a registered place in the typed ontology, with its training data and failure modes declared alongside every other property.
