Methodology

From observation to overlookedness

A latent-variable model for ranking humanitarian crises under incompleteness, temporal drift, and multi-stakeholder ambiguity.

Also available as a paper: proposal.pdf

§ 1The problem

When nearly every active humanitarian response is underfunded, a single coverage ratio no longer separates the worst-off from the rest. We need a way to distinguish how a crisis is overlooked, and for whom.

The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks $\$33$ billion to reach 135 million people. The 2025 appeal raised $\$12$ billion — the lowest in a decade. Nineteen country contexts now sit below fifty per cent coverage. The analytical consequence is direct: if most active responses are underfunded, sorting them by coverage ratio puts them all in a dense band together and tells you nothing useful about where to direct the next dollar.

Three kinds of overlookedness become distinguishable once scoring moves from a scalar to a joint distribution. A crisis can be forgotten — low funding, low attention, as with the 2023 Angola drought. It can be contested — high attention, persistent funding decline, as with Sudan in 2025. And it can be sector-starved — adequate aggregate coverage masking one sector at twenty per cent while another is overfunded. Each warrants a different operational response. Current tools collapse all three into one number.

§ 2A latent we cannot see

For each country $u$ at time $t$ there is a single unobserved quantity $\theta(u, t)$ we call overlookedness. We never observe it directly. We observe six attributes that are noisy, possibly missing, functions of it.

This is a statistical inference problem, not a scoring problem. The quantity of interest is latent. Our job is to infer its posterior distribution given the available evidence, and to be honest about how much we know when the evidence is sparse. We make four commitments about the nature of $\theta$.

  • $\theta$ is scalar, not vector. A country has one overlookedness value at a given time. A vector-valued latent is more expressive but requires a combination rule to obtain a scalar ranking, which re-introduces the weighted-sum problem we are trying to dissolve.
  • $\theta$ is unbounded. There is no finite maximum overlookedness; we do not impose one. The numerical scale is book-keeping. Everything operationally useful is a rank, or a credible interval on a rank.
  • $\theta$ is not identified in absolute terms. The model is invariant under location shifts and sign flips. We fix location by centring $\mathbb{E}_u[\theta(u, t)] = 0$ and sign by requiring the coverage-shortfall slope to be positive (higher shortfall should correspond to greater overlookedness).
  • $\theta$ describes the present and the past, not a prediction. Forecasting is the subject of a separate learning layer, described in § 10.
What this frames away from
We do not try to produce a single definitive "overlookedness score". We produce a posterior distribution over the latent per country, and let the width of that posterior do the work of expressing uncertainty. A single number, however clever the aggregation, cannot.

§ 3How attributes project onto $\theta$

Each observed attribute has its own likelihood — matched to its support, linked to $\theta$ through a slope and an intercept. Missing observations contribute no likelihood term at all.

The six attributes live on four different kinds of support: bounded fractions, positive reals, ordinal categories, and sparsely-observed cells. We do not squash them onto a common scale. Each gets the generalised-linear-model parameterisation it deserves.

Beta regression for bounded fractions

Four attributes — coverage shortfall, need intensity, donor concentration, and sector-coverage Gini — live on the unit interval. We give them a beta regression with a logit link:

$$ a_i(u, t) \mid \theta(u, t) \;\sim\; \mathrm{Beta}\bigl(\mu_i\,\phi_i,\; (1 - \mu_i)\,\phi_i\bigr), \qquad \mu_i \;=\; \sigma\bigl(\alpha_i + \beta_i\,\theta(u, t)\bigr). $$

The slope $\beta_i$ is the sensitivity of attribute $i$ to overlookedness on the logit scale; the precision $\phi_i$ controls dispersion. The logit link keeps predicted means strictly inside $(0, 1)$, as the attribute's support demands.

Log-normal for the per-PIN gap

The unmet funding per person in need is positive and spans orders of magnitude across crises. The natural model is multiplicative: $\log a_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_2 + \beta_2\,\theta,\; \sigma_2^2)$.

Ordered probit for severity

INFORM severity is a $\{1, \ldots, 5\}$ ordinal — Very Low through Very High. Treating it as a continuous 1 – 5 would imply the steps are equally spaced, which the qualitative descriptions suggest is false. An ordered probit lets the data recover the spacing. Introduce a latent continuous $y = \alpha_4 + \beta_4\,\theta + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, and four cut-points $\kappa_1 < \kappa_2 < \kappa_3 < \kappa_4$. The observed category $a_4 = k$ whenever $y$ lies in $(\kappa_{k-1}, \kappa_k]$, with $\kappa_0 = -\infty$ and $\kappa_5 = +\infty$. Cut-points are parameters we infer.

Missing observations

If an attribute is not observed for a given country-month, no likelihood term is contributed for that cell. No imputation is performed, no default value substituted. The posterior on $\theta(u, t)$ for a country-month with only two attributes observed will be wider than for one with six — as panel (d) above illustrates. This is not a procedural choice; it is a direct consequence of conditional probability. Incompleteness cannot produce a misleadingly narrow posterior.

§ 4Time and drift

A crisis does not reshuffle itself every month. Overlookedness has a stable country baseline with transient deviations around it. We model both explicitly.

For each country $u$, the trajectory $\theta(u, \cdot)$ is an AR(1) process with country-specific mean, persistence, and innovation scale:

$$ \theta(u, t) \;=\; \mu_u \;+\; \rho_u\bigl(\theta(u, t-1) - \mu_u\bigr) \;+\; \eta_u(t), \qquad \eta_u(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \tau_u^2). $$

Three things fall out. $\mu_u$ is the country's chronic overlookedness — its long-run baseline. The quantity $\theta(u, t) - \mu_u$ is the acute component — how the current month diverges from that baseline. And $\rho_u$ says how quickly shocks decay toward the baseline: near 1, drift persists; near 0, every month is a fresh draw.

A known weakness
AR(1) with constant $\mu_u$ assumes a stable baseline. Countries in sustained decline (Venezuela post-2018, Sudan post-2023) have a rising $\mu_u$; the AR(1) residual absorbs that drift rather than representing it. If this becomes visible in diagnostics, the fix is to let $\mu_u$ carry a linear trend or replace AR(1) with a local-level state-space model.

§ 5Stakeholders as priors

A stakeholder is not a weight vector. A stakeholder is a prior over how the six attributes project onto overlookedness. Four stakeholder priors, four posteriors, four views of the same latent.

Here the model acquires its novelty. Instead of asking "what weight should severity have in a weighted sum?", we ask "what does this stakeholder believe about the relationship between severity and overlookedness, a priori?". A stakeholder $s$ is a prior on the six slopes:

$$ \beta_i^{(s)} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(m_i^{(s)},\; v_i^{(s)}\bigr), \qquad i = 1, \ldots, 6. $$

Four pre-registered prior tables are given to the model, one per stakeholder. The mean vector $m^{(s)}$ encodes what the stakeholder cares most about; the variance vector $v^{(s)}$ encodes how strongly they hold that belief.

What this buys that weight vectors do not

Falsifiability. Each prior is a statistical claim. The posterior either corroborates it or moves away from it. If CERF's prior on the coverage slope is centred at $1.5$ but the data pool the posterior toward $0.8$, we have learned something concrete about CERF's stated-versus-effective preference. No weighted-sum method can produce a finding of that shape.

Uncertainty quantification. Stakeholder disagreement is no longer a four-point spread across fixed weights. It is four overlapping — or non-overlapping — posterior distributions over $\theta$.

Data-density sensitivity. When a country has rich data, the posterior on $\theta$ is dominated by the likelihood and all four stakeholder posteriors tend to converge. When a country has sparse data, the prior has more influence, and the stakeholders' disagreement shows up more sharply. The method is honestly context-sensitive.

§ 6Consensus vs contested

Having four posteriors turns "how much do stakeholders disagree?" from a heuristic into a measurement.

With one posterior $p(\theta(u, t) \mid D, s)$ per stakeholder, we can measure overlap directly. A country is a consensus case when all four posteriors concentrate on the same region; it is contested when they separate. Two simple scalar measures suffice.

  • Consensus score. The fraction of posterior samples in which the country is in the top-$K$ under all four stakeholder posteriors. High consensus score: no stakeholder disagrees this country is badly overlooked.
  • Contested score. The maximum of the posterior medians across stakeholders minus the minimum, or equivalently the Bhattacharyya distance between the most and least concordant posteriors. High contested score: the answer depends sharply on whose preferences you apply.

§ 7What the system reports

Every ranking carries its uncertainty. Coordinators see medians, 90 % credible intervals, and the attribute contributions that moved each posterior.

From $S$ posterior samples of $\theta(u, t)$ under a given stakeholder, the dashboard produces:

  • Posterior median $\widehat{\theta}(u, t)$ — the point estimate used for sorting.
  • 90 % credible interval on $\theta$ and on the rank within the scored pool — the honest statement of uncertainty.
  • Attribute contribution $\beta_i \cdot \tilde{a}_i(u, t)$ at the posterior medians, per attribute — the interpretable decomposition of what drove the rank.
  • Consensus and contested scores aggregated across stakeholders.

§ 8Validation

Three tests, stated in advance: temporal held-out, attribute masking, and consistency with the simpler method we shipped before.

  1. Temporal held-out. Fit on data through December 2024, predict posterior ranks for 2025, compare against OCHA's CERF Underfunded Emergencies selections for 2025. Top-$K$ overlap and rank correlation, each reported with a 90 % credible interval derived from the posterior samples.
  2. Attribute masking. For a country with full six-attribute observation, mask each attribute in turn and refit. The credible interval on the posterior rank should widen predictably. If masking an attribute narrows the interval, the model is miscalibrated.
  3. MAUT consistency. Under a deliberately diffuse stakeholder prior — large variance on all slopes — the posterior median ranking should approximately reproduce the weighted-sum ranking we shipped in the first version of the system. If it does not, at least one of them is wrong; we investigate before trusting either.

Validation is reported in the dashboard as a dedicated view and repeated in the formal paper for the record.

§ 9What we are not claiming

The system produces a ranked posterior, traceable to source data, with declared uncertainty. It does not do more than that.

  • Not forecasting. The model describes $\theta(u, t)$ at observed $t$. Forward prediction is the subject of the separate learning phase.
  • Not causal. The observation model is a statistical association. It does not support counterfactual claims of the form "if this country had more donors, it would be less overlooked".
  • Not a replacement for judgement. The system ranks; humans decide. Every displayed posterior carries the attribute contributions that produced it, so a coordinator can see exactly what drove a given answer.
  • Benchmarks are imperfect proxies. CERF Underfunded Emergencies selections reflect both genuine overlookedness and a human political process. CARE's Breaking the Silence reflects media attention. Agreement with either is informative, not conclusive.
  • Stakeholder priors are judgement, not calibration. The tables encode what we believe CERF, ECHO, USAID, and NGO consortia broadly care about. They are a starting point. Calibrating each prior against actual observed selections is a follow-on project.
  • Never directly observed. $\theta$ is latent. All posterior statements are contingent on the observation model being approximately correct. The validation tests above are the main evidence that it is; they are not proof.

§ 10Where this goes next

The typed semantic layer is a feature store. The next step is a learned representation of the raw monthly panel, slotted under the latent-variable model without disturbing it.

The six attributes we condition on are aggregates of a richer raw signal — eighteen INFORM sub-indicators on displacement, casualties, access, phase-level need, plus the monthly reporting on funding flows. A learned encoder over the raw monthly panels — a sequence model such as Mamba, or a masked-attention encoder trained by self-supervision — can compress that history into a low-dimensional $z(u, t) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ that carries information the aggregates do not.

The extension is architecturally straightforward. Replace the current observation model $a_i \mid \theta$ by $\{a, z\} \mid \theta$, with $z$ produced by the learned encoder. The latent $\theta$ stays scalar and interpretable. The learned encoder has a crisp, measurable training objective — reduce the posterior variance on $\theta$ given the available data. And the typed ontology carries through: every learned dimension of $z$ is registered as a new property at a dedicated level, with its training data, its checkpoint, and its known failure modes declared alongside everything else.

When that layer is in place, the system will produce posterior ranks conditioned on both structured attributes and learned representations, both provenance-traceable, both uncertainty-quantified, and both honest about what they do not know.
