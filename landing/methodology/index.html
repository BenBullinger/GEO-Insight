Methodology

From observation to overlookedness

A latent-variable model for ranking humanitarian crises under incompleteness, temporal drift, and multi-stakeholder ambiguity.

Also available as a paper: proposal.pdf

§ 1The problem

Sorting humanitarian crises by funding coverage used to work. It no longer does, because almost every active response is now in the failure zone together.

The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks $\$33$ billion to reach 135 million people. The 2025 appeal raised $\$12$ billion — the lowest in a decade. Nineteen country contexts sit below fifty per cent coverage, up from eight in 2021.

A single coverage ratio places all of them in a dense band near the floor. It doesn't distinguish between the three faces a neglected crisis can have: forgotten (unfunded and unnoticed — Angola 2023), contested (unfunded despite global attention — Sudan 2025), or sector-starved (adequate in aggregate while one sector sits at twenty per cent). Three problems, three different responses. The rest of this document describes a model that separates them.

§ 2A quantity we cannot see

The thing we want to measure — how overlooked a crisis is — is not written down anywhere. We have to infer it.

Call it $\theta(u, t)$: one real number per country $u$ per month $t$. It is a fiction — no one has ever written it down — but a useful one, because it lets us ask a sharper question than which crisis has the lowest coverage ratio. The sharper question is: given the evidence we do have, what does the data say about $\theta$, and how confident should we be?

The reframe
This turns the problem from scoring into inference. Our output is not a number — it is a posterior distribution over $\theta$ per country, whose width honestly reports what the evidence can and cannot tell us. A single number, however cleverly aggregated, cannot carry that uncertainty.

§ 3The clues we see

Six clues. Four different shapes. Each gets an equation matched to what it actually is.

We work with six: funding coverage shortfall, unmet amount per person in need, fraction of the population in need, INFORM severity category, donor concentration, and how evenly funding is split across sectors. Three live on $[0, 1]$. One is a positive real spanning orders of magnitude. One is an ordinal $\{1, \ldots, 5\}$. Any of them can be missing.

For the bounded fractions, beta regression keeps the predicted mean on $[0, 1]$: $a_i \mid \theta \sim \mathrm{Beta}\bigl(\mu_i\phi_i,\,(1-\mu_i)\phi_i\bigr)$ with $\mu_i = \sigma(\alpha_i + \beta_i\theta)$. For the per-person gap, $\log a_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_2 + \beta_2\theta,\,\sigma_2^2)$. For severity, ordered logistic with four cut-points $\kappa_1 < \kappa_2 < \kappa_3 < \kappa_4$ we infer alongside everything else — we let the data say how INFORM actually spaces its labels. The slopes $\beta_i$ are sign-constrained positive throughout: every attribute is defined so that larger values mean more overlooked.

For missing observations, nothing. No imputation. The likelihood contribution is simply zero. This is not a convention — it falls out of conditional probability. And it has a consequence the whole system depends on: a country with sparse data produces a wider posterior, never a confidently extreme one. Fig 2(d) shows exactly this.

§ 4Crises have memory

Sudan in April 2025 is not independent of Sudan in March 2025. The model should know this. The shape of that knowledge is an AR(1) per country.

A country's overlookedness has a baseline and has episodes. The honest way to say so is:

$$ \theta(u, t) \;=\; \mu_u \;+\; \rho_u\bigl(\theta(u, t-1) - \mu_u\bigr) \;+\; \eta_u(t), \qquad \eta_u(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \tau_u^2). $$

Three quantities fall out of the same posterior. $\mu_u$ is the country's long-run baseline — the chronic component. The deviation $\theta(u, t) - \mu_u$ is the acute component — how this month compares to the country's usual level. $\rho_u$ says how long shocks persist.

What is and isn't shipped
The cross-sectional fit (§3) is shipped and externally validated. The AR(1) extension above is the next layer: it requires more time points than the annual HRP/FTS attributes give us. INFORM severity is monthly and would anchor a partial-temporal model. In the meantime, fitting the cross-sectional model on consecutive cycles already gives a 7/10 year-over-year top-10 overlap and a held-out 4/10 against CERF UFE March-2025 from a 2024-only fit.

§ 5Stakeholders, as priors

Each stakeholder is a probability distribution over how strongly the six attributes reflect overlookedness. Different beliefs produce different posteriors over the same data.

CERF emphasises severity and coverage shortfall. ECHO leans on equity. USAID looks at donor concentration. NGO consortia weight sector-equity. Each stakeholder $s$ is encoded as a prior over the six attribute slopes:

$$ \beta_i^{(s)} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(m_i^{(s)},\; v_i^{(s)}\bigr), \qquad i = 1, \ldots, 6. $$

The mean $m_i^{(s)}$ says what the stakeholder believes a priori; the variance $v_i^{(s)}$ says how tightly. Each stakeholder produces a separate posterior over $\theta$.

Three things follow. First, the priors are falsifiable: after fitting, the posterior on $\beta$ either corroborates a stakeholder's belief or moves away from it. If CERF's prior centres the coverage slope at 1.5 but the data pull the posterior to 0.8, we have learned something concrete about CERF's stated-versus-effective preference. Second, stakeholder disagreement is the overlap of four posterior distributions, which we can measure directly — not a four-point spread to be reconciled. Third, the method is honestly context-sensitive: where data is abundant, all four posteriors converge; where it is sparse, stakeholder differences show up more. The system adapts to how much evidence it has.

§ 6What comes out

Every rank comes with its uncertainty. The posterior median is the point estimate; the 90 % credible interval is the honest statement of what the evidence can support.

For each HRP-eligible country, we read posterior samples to extract: a posterior median $\widehat\theta$, a 90 % credible interval on $\theta$, and (with stakeholder priors plugged in) a per-stakeholder posterior whose overlap defines consensus and contested. The latent is best read as a funding-overlookedness axis: posterior predictive checks (next section) show coverage shortfall and donor concentration drive the latent most strongly, while need intensity and severity sit near attribute ceilings and constrain the posterior without differentiating between countries.

With four stakeholder posteriors per country, we can measure how much they overlap. A country is consensus when all four distributions concentrate on the same range; it is contested when they separate. The consensus score is the fraction of posterior samples in which the country is in the top-$K$ under all four stakeholders simultaneously. The contested score is the spread of posterior medians across stakeholders. Fig 5 shows one country of each kind.

§ 7Validation, and what we are not claiming

Three validations. External benchmarks, posterior predictive checks, temporal held-out. Each falsifies a different claim; each must pass on its own terms.

External benchmarks. The model's posterior median ranking is scored against two human-curated lists, both produced by methodology entirely independent of anything in this paper: CERF Underfunded Emergencies (the UN Emergency Relief Coordinator's twice-yearly allocations) and CARE Breaking the Silence (annual top-ten most under-reported crises). The Bayesian model ties an additive baseline on the larger CERF windows and beats it on the smaller windows and on CARE BTS, where the baseline collapses.

Benchmark $k$ Bayesian Additive baseline
CERF UFE 2024 w2 (Dec 2024) 10 3/10 3/10
CERF UFE 2025 w1 (Mar 2025) 10 5/10 5/10
CERF UFE 2025 w2 (Dec 2025) 7 2/7 1/7
CARE BTS 2024 10 3/10 1/10

Posterior predictive checks. For each of the six attributes, we draw 1{,}000 replicates from the fitted posterior and overlay them on the observed marginal. Coverage of the 90 % predictive interval is at or above 0.91 for every attribute (target 0.90) — the model's marginals are calibrated. The per-country Pearson correlation between predicted mean and observed value is high for coverage shortfall (0.76) and donor concentration (0.82), and weaker for need intensity, intra-crisis equity, and severity — attributes that sit near their ceilings (most HRP countries have PIN $\approx$ population and severity $\in \{4, 5\}$) and so carry little cross-country variation for the latent to recover.

Temporal held-out. Fit the model on the 2024 cycle alone (HRP 2024, FTS through end-2024, INFORM through end-2024); score the 2024-fit ranking against CERF UFE selections made in 2025. The 2024 fit predicts CERF's March-2025 selection at 4/10 precision @ 10 — without seeing any 2025 data. Year-over-year top-10 overlap is 7/10. The model identifies the same crisis cluster across cycles; turnover at the boundary reflects real cycle-to-cycle shifts.

Calibration. The variational posterior is checked against NUTS, the gold-standard MCMC reference, on every fit. The production AutoMultivariateNormal guide runs in three seconds and recovers NUTS posterior medians at Spearman $\rho = 0.89$, with credible intervals within $2\times$ of NUTS. The simpler mean-field AutoNormal guide gives $\rho = 0.68$ and underestimates posterior variance by ${\sim}3\times$ — a finding that surfaced only because we ran NUTS, and the reason we ship the multivariate guide.

What the system does is produce ranked posteriors, traceable to source data, with declared uncertainty. What it does not do is equally important. It does not forecast — the temporal layer above is the handle for that. It does not make causal claims; the observation model is association, not intervention. It does not replace judgement; it produces a ranked list, a coordinator decides. The benchmarks themselves are imperfect proxies, reflecting political process and media attention. The stakeholder priors are judgement, not calibration; regressing actual selections on the attributes to recover a stakeholder's effective prior is a natural extension we have not done yet. And fundamentally, $\theta$ is never directly observed — every posterior statement is contingent on the observation model being approximately correct. Validation gives us evidence. It does not give us proof.

§ 8The learned layer

The six signals above are themselves aggregates. The information they throw away is what the next layer will carry.

Under our six aggregates sits a richer raw panel: eighteen INFORM sub-indicators on displacement, casualties, access, and phase-level need; monthly flows donor by donor; sector-level breakdowns. A sequence model trained on that panel — Mamba, a masked-attention encoder — can compress the raw history into an embedding $z(u, t)$ that carries what the aggregates cannot. The extension slots in cleanly: replace $a_i \mid \theta$ with $\{a, z\} \mid \theta$. The latent $\theta$ stays scalar and interpretable. The encoder has a crisp objective — reduce posterior variance on $\theta$ given the evidence — and a registered place in the typed ontology, with its training data and failure modes declared alongside every other property.
