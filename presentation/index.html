The analytical problem

When every response is underfunded,
"underfunded" stops being useful.

A single coverage ratio can't discriminate between 19 contexts that are all below 50 %. The interesting variation is no longer whether a crisis is underfunded — but how, and for whom.

How the score works

For each crisis, six signals are weighted by a donor profile and aggregated.

\[G_p(u) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{6} w_{p,i} \cdot U_i\!\left(\tilde{a}_i(u)\right)\]
G_p(u)gap score for crisis u under profile p
w_{p,i}profile p's weight on signal i
U_i(·)utility transform for signal i
ã_i(u)normalised signal value for u

Six signals: coverage shortfall · per-PIN funding gap · need intensity · severity · donor concentration · intra-crisis equity Gini (new).
Four profiles: CERF-, ECHO-, USAID-, NGO-flavoured.

GEO-Insight · Cache Me if You Can7

A third axis: time

A crisis overlooked for five years needs different policy than one that just tipped. We separate the two.

Chronic

Persistent underfunding across years. Response: multi-year pooled funding.

Acute

Recent collapse from baseline. Response: CERF rapid-response window.

Chronic = $k$-year rolling mean of the gap. Acute = deviation of this year from that rolling mean.

What this is not

NOT AUTOMATION

The tool ranks. Humans decide. A score is a hypothesis, never a verdict.

NOT A SINGLE NUMBER

Four profiles, four ranks. We always report the median and the IQR.

NOT IMPUTED

Stale HNO, missing clusters, plan-less crises are flagged — never silently filled.

NOT NEUTRAL

We surface donor disagreement as signal, instead of hiding it behind consensus.

NOT COMPLETE

~20 countries are full-stack. The rest degrade gracefully; we say so.

NOT PRECISE

Two significant figures. A score of 0.84 is not meaningfully distinct from 0.82.
