\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Humanitarian coordinators and donor advisors need rapid, defensible answers to one question: \emph{where does documented need most outpace available funding?} The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks \$33B for 135M people, yet the 2025 appeal raised only \$12B --- the lowest in a decade. Nineteen country contexts are now severely underfunded, up from eight in 2021. When almost every response is underfunded, the useful question shifts from \emph{which crises are funded?} to \emph{relative to documented need, which crises are most overlooked?}

Existing tools --- CERF UFE rankings, FTS coverage ratios, and Palantir-Foundry-based systems such as WFP's DOTS --- treat overlookedness as a \textbf{scalar}: one number per country-year. Our contribution is a \emph{joint-distribution} view of overlookedness, anchored in three explicit research gaps in the 2015--2026 humanitarian-analytics literature. We extend the MADM/AHP/MAUT framework of Rye \& Aktas~\cite{rye2022} with two new axes:
(i) an \textbf{intra-crisis equity term} measuring inequality of coverage across sector clusters within a single country, following the fair-distribution formulation of Vargas Florez et al.~\cite{vargasflorez2015};
(ii) an \textbf{inter-stakeholder disagreement term} measuring rank variance across four donor-preference profiles, operationalising the per-decision-maker rank divergence empirically demonstrated by Rye \& Aktas (Table~13).
The resulting four-cell typology --- \emph{consensus-overlooked}, \emph{contested}, \emph{sector-starved}, or combinations --- is a classification that current tools conflate. Validated against CERF Underfunded Emergencies (consensus axis) and CARE \emph{Breaking the Silence} (sector-starved axis).
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

The humanitarian system in 2026 is operating under unprecedented fiscal strain. According to OCHA's Global Humanitarian Overview 2026,\footnote{\url{https://www.unocha.org/publications/report/world/global-humanitarian-overview-2026-enesfr}} agencies are asking \$33B to reach 135 million people, with a hyper-prioritised \$23B floor targeting 87 million lives. The 2025 appeal raised only \$12B. Headline coverage ratios are collapsing: DRC $\sim$22\%, Yemen and Somalia $\sim$24\%, Sudan from 69\% (2024) to 35\% (2025).\footnote{\url{https://www.thenewhumanitarian.org/news/2025/12/08/five-takeaways-uns-aid-plans-2026}} OCHA now flags 19 contexts as severely underfunded (below 50\% coverage), up from eight in 2021 --- severe underfunding is the norm, not the exception.

The analytical implication: scoring overlookedness with a single coverage ratio is no longer discriminative. \textcolor{red}{When 19 contexts are all under 50\% coverage, the interesting variation is no longer \emph{whether} a crisis is underfunded but \emph{how} and \emph{for whom}.}

\section{Problem Definition}

Given a query or geographic scope (country, region, sector, or natural-language prompt), GEO-Insight returns a ranked list of crises. Each ranked entry carries:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item a \textbf{median gap score} across four donor-preference profiles;
    \item an \textbf{inter-profile disagreement band} (rank inter-quartile range across profiles);
    \item an \textbf{intra-crisis equity score} (cluster-coverage Gini);
    \item a \textbf{four-cell typology label} (Section~\ref{sec:fourcell});
    \item an attribute-level decomposition of the score;
    \item data-quality flags (stale HNO, incomplete funding data, no active HRP);
    \item a brief natural-language explanation suitable for a briefing note.
\end{itemize}

Ambiguous queries are handled by graceful degradation. Outputs do not fabricate figures absent from the source data. Uncertainty is surfaced rather than suppressed.

\section{Related Work and the Gap We Close}

Three strands of literature shape the methodology. The synthesis below emphasises \emph{convergent language across papers} about what the field is missing.

\paragraph{Multi-attribute decision-making for humanitarian allocation.}
Rye \& Aktas~\cite{rye2022} propose EDIS, a decision-support framework using multi-attribute decision making (MADM), AHP for preference elicitation, and MAUT for aggregation. Their Table~13 explicitly demonstrates supplier-rank divergence under different decision-maker preferences --- but stops short of aggregating this divergence into a meta-metric. Their Section~7 flags ``actual non-resource-based determinants of supplier selection'' as open.

\paragraph{Three papers converge on equity as the open gap.}
Abdulrashid et al.~\cite{abdulrashid2026}, in a 2026 systematic review of AI-DSS for disaster management, state (\S7.3): \emph{``Despite the growing recognition that humanitarian logistics decisions often have ethical implications, few studies incorporate equity-aware or fairness-oriented modelling.''} Table~12 lists ``How can recovery-focused AI-DSS incorporate equity metrics into rebuilding prioritisation?'' as one of only five items in its research agenda.

Sahebjamnia et al.~\cite{sahebjamnia2017}, concluding their hybrid-DSS design, name the same gap (\S6, p.~22): \emph{``using more performance measures \ldots involving several egalitarian and utilitarian measures is of vital importance.''}

Vargas Florez et al.~\cite{vargasflorez2015} give the equation template. Their Eqs.~9--12 introduce a fair-distribution penalty on the maximum regional shortage percentage $PM(s) = \max_i R(i,s) / d(i,s)$, where $R$ is unmet requirement and $d$ is demand. The efficiency objective, they note explicitly, \emph{``conflicts with the criterion of fair distribution.''} We generalise this formulation from inter-regional to inter-cluster inequality within a single country response.

\paragraph{Embedded, interpretable analytics.}
Griffith et al.~\cite{griffith2017} argue that during time-constrained humanitarian operations, simple and interpretable analytics outperform complex black-box methods, and characterise the field as ``data rich, information poor'' with data integration (not algorithm choice) as the operational bottleneck. We take this as a constraint on any method that adds to the score vector: every new term must decompose cleanly and be explainable to a non-technical analyst.

\paragraph{What GEO-Insight adds that is not in this literature.}
No paper in our review aggregates per-decision-maker rank divergence into an operational ``disagreement'' meta-score. No paper operationalises intra-crisis equity as a Gini-type inequality on cluster coverage ratios within a single country HRP. Both are small, defensible extensions of existing frameworks. Neither is addressed by Palantir-Foundry-based systems such as WFP's DOTS~\cite{wfppalantir}, which unify an organisation's operational data toward a single source of truth --- the opposite of surfacing inter-stakeholder disagreement.

\subsection{Gaps and public-source workarounds}

Three items needed by the methodology are not on disk. All three are openly available online.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=3pt,topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Severity ($a_4$) --- solved at global scale via INFORM Severity.} HNO 2024/2025 CSVs do not carry a structured severity score. We therefore fold in the EU JRC's INFORM Severity Index from \url{https://drmkc.jrc.ec.europa.eu/inform-index/INFORM-Severity/Results-and-data}: a 1--10 composite assessed monthly for every active humanitarian crisis worldwide. All 67 monthly snapshots (2020-09 to 2026-03) are downloaded by \texttt{Data/Third-Party/DRMKC-INFORM/download.py} and consolidated by \texttt{consolidate.py} into a single long-format CSV with 5,298 (country\,$\times$\,month) rows covering 109 countries; every full-stack priority country has $\geq$60 months of history. This is load-bearing in two ways: it resolves the $a_4$ attribute globally, and it supplies the panel-data backbone that makes the temporal bonus task tractable (state-space filtering, hierarchical baselines). An earlier IPC export remains at \texttt{Data/Third-Party/IPCInfo/} as a richer single-country backup (Afghanistan, 17 analyses 2017--2025) for demo purposes.
    \item \textbf{CERF UFE benchmark.} Annual list published on CERF's website; scraped manually once per cycle. $\sim$30 entries per year, trivial to transcribe.
    \item \textbf{CARE \emph{Breaking the Silence}.} Annual PDF on CARE and ReliefWeb; top-10 list is extracted manually.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Required pre-processing}\label{sec:preproc}

Two concrete engineering steps are required before the scoring pipeline can run.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=3pt,topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Cluster-taxonomy harmonisation.} HNO and FTS label identical sector clusters inconsistently --- HNO ``Sanitation \& Hygiene'' vs.\ FTS ``Water Sanitation and Hygiene'' vs.\ the French ``EHA - Eau Hygi\`ene Assainissement.'' We maintain a manual mapping table of $\sim$20--30 entries at \texttt{src/taxonomies/cluster\_map.csv} to canonicalise cluster names before joining on \texttt{(country, cluster, year)}. Without this mapping, $a_6$ collapses silently.
    \item \textbf{Multi-country FTS allocation for HHI.} Some FTS incoming records have pipe-delimited \texttt{destLocations} spanning multiple countries (regional plans). We apply the following rule: if the destination plan code unambiguously identifies a lead country (e.g.\ \texttt{HAFG25} $\to$ AFG) the funding is attributed to that country; otherwise the amount is split equally across listed destinations and the resulting $a_5$ is flagged ``estimated.''
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Verdict}

All six score attributes and both validation benchmarks are recoverable from public sources. Severity is the only attribute requiring an external fetch; IPC provides it. No attribute is non-computable. \textbf{The methodology is feasible under the available data} --- the binding constraint is engineering time on the two pre-processing tasks above plus one IPC fetch, not data existence.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Unit of analysis}

The primary unit is the \emph{country-level crisis response} for an active HRP cycle. The intra-crisis equity term (Section~\ref{sec:equity}) requires sector-level decomposition; where HNO/FTS sector breakdowns are absent or stale, the equity term degrades to a null value and is flagged rather than imputed.

\subsection{Eligibility filtering}

A crisis $u$ enters the ranking pool only if:
(1) an active HRP exists \emph{or} documented PIN exceeds $\tau_{\text{PIN}}$ (default $10^6$);
(2) HNO figures are no older than $\tau_{\text{stale}}$ months (default 24);
(3) at least one FTS funding record exists for the current or prior plan year.
Crises failing (1) are reported in a secondary ``possible blind-spot'' list; not scored against HRP-backed crises.

\subsection{Attribute vector (six attributes)}\label{sec:attributes}

For each eligible crisis $u$, let $P(u)$ denote people in need, $R(u)$ funding requirements, $F(u)$ funding received, $N(u)$ country population, $D(u)$ the vector of per-donor contributions, and for each sector cluster $c$, let $r_c(u) = F_c(u)/R_c(u)$ be the cluster coverage ratio and $P_c(u)$ be cluster-level PIN. Then:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
a_1(u) &= 1 - \min(1,\; F(u)/R(u)) && \text{coverage shortfall}\\
a_2(u) &= \max(0,\; R(u)-F(u))/P(u) && \text{per-PIN funding gap (USD)}\\
a_3(u) &= P(u)/N(u) && \text{need intensity}\\
a_4(u) &= \text{severity}(u) \in [1,5] && \text{HNO severity / IPC phase}\\
a_5(u) &= \text{HHI}(D(u)) && \text{donor concentration}\\
a_6(u) &= G_{\text{cluster}}(u) && \text{intra-crisis equity (new)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Intra-crisis equity term $a_6$}\label{sec:equity}

Following the fair-distribution logic of Vargas Florez et al.~\cite{vargasflorez2015} (Eqs.~9--12), we measure inequality of coverage across sector clusters within a single country's response. Let $\mathcal{C}(u)$ be the set of clusters with non-null FTS and HNO figures. The PIN-weighted Gini coefficient of coverage is:

\begin{equation}
G_{\text{cluster}}(u) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{C}} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{C}} P_i(u)\, P_j(u)\, |r_i(u) - r_j(u)|}{2\,\bigl(\sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} P_c(u)\bigr)^2 \; \bar{r}(u)}
\qquad \bar{r}(u) = \frac{\sum_c P_c(u)\, r_c(u)}{\sum_c P_c(u)}
\label{eq:gini}
\end{equation}

$G_{\text{cluster}}(u) \in [0,1]$ with $0$ meaning all clusters equally funded and $1$ meaning total concentration. A crisis with aggregate coverage 80\% but a Health cluster at 20\% carries a large $a_6$ and is flagged as \emph{sector-starved}. This directly operationalises the ``egalitarian measures'' gap named by Sahebjamnia et al.~\cite{sahebjamnia2017} (\S6) and Abdulrashid et al.~\cite{abdulrashid2026} (\S7.3, Table~12).

An alternative Vargas-Florez-faithful formulation tracks the maximum per-PIN shortage across clusters:
\[
a_6'(u) = \max_{c \in \mathcal{C}(u)} (R_c(u) - F_c(u))/P_c(u).
\]
We treat $a_6'$ as a sensitivity check; the Gini is the primary choice because its $[0,1]$ bound makes it directly compatible with the MAUT utility transform.

\subsection{Normalisation and utility}

Each attribute is min-max normalised over the eligible pool to $\tilde{a}_i(u) \in [0,1]$, oriented so that larger values indicate ``more overlooked.'' Per-attribute utility $U_i$ is linear by default; for coverage shortfall ($a_1$) we use concave $U_1(x) = 1 - (1-x)^\gamma$, $\gamma \in [1.5, 2.5]$, to amplify the low-coverage tail (moving from 10\% to 5\% coverage matters more than moving from 60\% to 55\%).

\subsection{Four donor-preference profiles}\label{sec:profiles}

Overlookedness is computed under four distinct weight vectors $w_p \in \Delta^5$, one per stylised donor profile. Weights are defaults, not claims about any specific institution's formal policy; they are tuneable and serve to expose how the ranking shifts under plausible mandates.

\subsection{Composite gap score and disagreement band}

Under profile $p$ the score is:
\begin{equation}
G_p(u) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{6} w_{p,i}\, U_i(\tilde{a}_i(u))
\label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}

For each crisis $u$ we then rank the pool under every profile and report:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item $\text{rank}_p(u)$ for each $p \in \{\text{CERF, ECHO, USAID, NGO}\}$;
    \item $\widetilde{\text{rank}}(u) = \mathrm{median}_p\, \text{rank}_p(u)$;
    \item $\text{IQR}(u) = \text{IQR}_p\, \text{rank}_p(u)$ --- the \emph{disagreement band}.
\end{itemize}

Small IQR on a high median rank indicates \emph{consensus overlooked}: every plausible donor mandate ranks this crisis highly. Large IQR indicates \emph{contested}: the crisis's priority depends on the weighting philosophy.

\subsection{Explanation layer}

The per-attribute contribution $c_{p,i}(u) = w_{p,i}\, U_i(\tilde{a}_i(u))$ decomposes the score under each profile (Griffith et al.~\cite{griffith2017} interpretability principle). For each top-$K$ crisis we report the dominant two attributes per profile, absolute reference figures (PIN, requirements, received, coverage), typology label, and data-quality caveats.

\subsection{Query layer}

Natural-language queries parse via a lightweight LLM call with fixed prompt and JSON schema. \emph{``Acute food insecurity hotspots with below 10\% funding''} $\Rightarrow$ \texttt{\{sector: "food", severity: $\geq 4$, coverage\_max: 0.10\}}. The LLM is not involved in scoring. Schema-validation failures fall back to keyword filtering with a degradation flag.

\section{Third Axis: Structural vs.\ Acute Neglect (Bonus)}\label{sec:bonus}

The typology in Section~\ref{sec:fourcell} is a point-in-time view. To address the bonus question --- distinguishing chronic from recent underfunding --- we add a third, temporal axis. Let $G_{p,t}(u)$ be the profile-$p$ score computed using data available at year $t$. For a rolling window of $k$ years:

\begin{align}
\bar{G}_{p,k}(u) &= \frac{1}{k}\sum_{t=T-k+1}^{T} G_{p,t}(u) && \text{chronic component}\\
\Delta G_p(u)    &= G_{p,T}(u) - \frac{1}{k-1}\sum_{t=T-k+1}^{T-1} G_{p,t}(u) && \text{acute component}
\end{align}

We average these over profiles and surface the (chronic, acute) pair as a 2-D view per crisis. A crisis in the \emph{consensus-overlooked, high-chronic} quadrant is a candidate for multi-year pooled funding; one in \emph{consensus-overlooked, high-acute} is a candidate for the CERF rapid-response window. This explicitly connects the policy recommendation to the ranking output.

\section{System Architecture}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Ingestion.} HDX HAPI client (preferred) with fallback to direct CSV pulls. Pandas dataframes cached locally with dataset version and fetch timestamp.
    \item \textbf{Harmonisation.} Country ISO-3 canonicalisation, sector taxonomy mapping (HNO clusters $\leftrightarrow$ FTS sector codes), plan-year alignment.
    \item \textbf{Scoring engine.} Pure-Python computation of Equations~(1)--(\ref{eq:gap}); deterministic, fully unit-testable. Gini via a vectorised pandas implementation.
    \item \textbf{Multi-profile driver.} Re-runs scoring under each $w_p$; aggregates $\widetilde{\text{rank}}$ and IQR.
    \item \textbf{Query layer.} Pydantic schema + LLM-assisted parser; fallback to keyword routing.
    \item \textbf{Presentation.} Notebook for development; Streamlit dashboard with four-cell scatter plot; JSON export.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Validation Strategy}

We validate against \emph{two independent benchmarks} --- one per axis.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Consensus axis (CERF UFE).} Our \emph{consensus-overlooked} list should overlap substantially with OCHA's CERF Underfunded Emergencies window selections.\footnote{\url{https://cerf.un.org/apply-for-a-grant/underfunded-emergencies}} Metrics: Overlap@10, Spearman $\rho$ between $\widetilde{\text{rank}}$ and CERF UFE inclusion order.
    \item \textbf{Sector-starved axis (CARE Breaking the Silence).} Our high-$G_{\text{cluster}}$ list should correlate with the top under-reported crises catalogued in CARE International's annual report.\footnote{\url{https://reliefweb.int/report/angola/breaking-silence-10-most-under-reported-humanitarian-crises-2023}} Metrics: Overlap@10, rank correlation.
    \item \textbf{Contested axis (qualitative).} The \emph{contested} list is hard to validate quantitatively but should align with crises known to exhibit donor tension (e.g.\ Venezuela, Ethiopia--Tigray, Myanmar). We report this as a qualitative cross-check, flagged as such.
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity.} How much does top-$K$ shift under leave-one-profile-out? Low shift indicates the consensus set is not an artefact of profile choice.
    \item \textbf{Stress tests.} Deliberate removal of sectors, years, or donor data to confirm graceful degradation and correct data-quality flags.
\end{itemize}

Two independent benchmarks on two orthogonal claims (consensus underfunding, and intra-crisis cluster inequality) materially strengthens the jury story over single-benchmark validation.

\section{Handling Uncertainty and Data Quality}

Following Abdulrashid et al.~\cite{abdulrashid2026}, we treat data-quality transparency as a first-class output.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Stale HNO figures.} Vintage carried through scoring; crises with PIN $>$24 months old flagged amber.
    \item \textbf{Funding definition.} We commit to \emph{funding received} as the numerator of coverage; all three (requested/pledged/received) reported in the per-crisis detail view.
    \item \textbf{Missing sector data.} Equity term degrades to null and is flagged, not imputed; the crisis then has a five-attribute score instead of six.
    \item \textbf{Plan-less active crises.} Reported separately (``possible blind-spot'' list), not merged into the main ranking.
    \item \textbf{Sparse donor counts.} HHI flagged as ``single-donor-risk'' categorical below two donors; not computed numerically.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}

\paragraph{Decision support, not automation.} The tool produces rankings; humans decide. A score is a hypothesis to investigate, not a verdict.
\paragraph{Visibility bias.} Datasets overrepresent crises with functioning information architectures. We surface this via evidence-age flags but cannot fully correct.
\paragraph{Profile weights are illustrative.} The four profiles in Section~\ref{sec:profiles} reflect stylised mandate emphases. No specific institution has endorsed any of them. We frame them as plausible sampling points in weight space, not as donor simulations.
\paragraph{Political sensitivity.} Declaring a crisis ``contested'' or ``overlooked'' is a claim with political implications. Outputs report numeric facts and attribute contributions, not donor-shaming narratives.
\paragraph{False precision.} Gap scores reported to two significant figures; disagreement IQR always shown. Scores of 0.84 and 0.82 are treated as equivalent.
\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Data fetches timestamped and versioned. External LLM calls logged.
