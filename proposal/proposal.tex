\title{\textbf{Geo-Insight}\\[0.25em]
  \normalsize\mdseries Overlookedness as Disagreement:\\
  A Multi-Attribute, Multi-Stakeholder Gap-Ranking System for Humanitarian Crises}
\author{\small Datathon 2026}
\date{\small April 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In 2026, agencies are asking \$33B to reach 135\,M people; the 2025 appeal raised \$12B, the lowest in a decade, with 19~contexts below 50\,\% coverage. When nearly every response is underfunded, a single coverage ratio stops discriminating. We recast overlookedness as a joint distribution: a coverage-gap score computed under four donor-preference profiles, plus an intra-crisis equity term (sector-coverage Gini) and a cross-stakeholder disagreement term (rank IQR across profiles). The resulting four-cell typology separates \emph{consensus-overlooked}, \emph{contested}, and \emph{sector-starved} crises that single-number methods conflate. Scoring is \emph{evidence-bounded}: missing signals contribute zero, so incomplete data cannot inflate rank. The method is implemented over a typed semantic layer of 71 properties across five derivation levels. Validated against two independently-curated benchmarks: 100\,\% top-5 overlap with OCHA's CERF Underfunded Emergencies on the strict pool ($n=18$), 80\,\% on the widest pool ($n=57$).
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks \$33B for 135\,M people, with a hyper-prioritised \$23B floor targeting 87\,M lives.\footnote{\url{https://www.unocha.org/publications/report/world/global-humanitarian-overview-2026-enesfr}} The 2025 appeal raised \$12B. Coverage ratios have collapsed~--- DRC $\sim$22\,\%, Yemen and Somalia $\sim$24\,\%, Sudan from 69\,\% (2024) to 35\,\% (2025). Nineteen contexts are flagged as severely underfunded, up from eight in 2021. A single coverage ratio cannot distinguish between 19 countries that are all below 50\,\% coverage; the useful variation is no longer \emph{whether} a crisis is underfunded but \emph{how}, and \emph{for whom}.

Three kinds of overlookedness become distinguishable once scoring moves from scalar to joint distribution: \emph{forgotten} (low funding, low attention~--- Angola drought 2023), \emph{contested} (high attention, persistent decline~--- Sudan 2025), and \emph{sector-starved} (adequate aggregate, one sector at 20\,\%). Each warrants a different response~--- multi-year pooled funding, targeted advocacy, sector-level reallocation~--- and current tools conflate them.

\section{Related Work}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Rye \& Aktas~\cite{rye2022} propose EDIS, a MADM/AHP/MAUT framework whose Table~13 demonstrates rank divergence under different decision-maker preferences but stops short of aggregating it into a meta-metric. Three papers converge on equity as the open gap. Abdulrashid et~al.~\cite{abdulrashid2026} (2026 review, \S7.3) list equity-aware prioritisation as one of only five open research items. Sahebjamnia et~al.~\cite{sahebjamnia2017} call for \emph{``egalitarian and utilitarian measures''}. Vargas Florez et~al.~\cite{vargasflorez2015} give the equation template (Eqs.~9--12) for fair-distribution penalties; we generalise from inter-regional to inter-cluster inequality within a single country response. Griffith et~al.~\cite{griffith2017} argue for interpretable analytics in time-constrained humanitarian operations, which we take as a hard constraint: every added term must decompose cleanly and be explainable to a non-technical analyst. No paper in the review aggregates per-decision-maker rank divergence into an operational disagreement meta-score, and none operationalises intra-crisis equity as a Gini on cluster coverage ratios within a single country response. Both are small, defensible extensions.

\section{Data}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Six upstream datasets, with declared provenance. Five from OCHA: HNO (people in need per country and sector), HRP (plan metadata), FTS (requirements, funding received, incoming flows per donor), CoD-PS (population), and CBPF (pooled-fund allocations and donor contributions). One from the EU Joint Research Centre: INFORM Severity, as 67 monthly global snapshots consolidated into long-format CSVs (109 countries, 1--5 ordinal severity plus sub-indicators: affected, displaced, fatalities, phase-level PIN splits, access-limited/restricted populations). Two externally-curated lists for validation only, not training: CERF Underfunded Emergencies (22 country selections across 2024--25) and CARE \emph{Breaking the Silence} (annual top-10 by media attention, not funding). Countries missing more than half of the six scoring signals are withheld rather than imputed.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textbf{Unit of analysis} is country-year. A country-year is eligible if it has either positive FTS requirements or a non-null 24-month INFORM severity baseline; it is \emph{scored} if completeness $c(u) \geq \tfrac{1}{2}$.

\textbf{Six-attribute vector} per country-year:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
  \item $a_1$ coverage shortfall $= 1 - \min(\text{fund}/\text{req}, 1)$
  \item $a_2$ gap per PIN $= \max(0, \text{req}-\text{fund})/\text{PIN}$
  \item $a_3$ need intensity $= \text{PIN}/\text{population}$
  \item $a_4$ severity: INFORM 1--5 ordinal
  \item $a_5$ donor concentration (HHI over FTS single-destination incoming flows)
  \item $a_6$ intra-crisis equity: PIN-weighted Gini over sector coverage ratios,
  \[
  a_6(u) = \frac{\sum_{i,j} n_i n_j |k_i - k_j|}{2\,(\sum_c n_c)^2\,\bar k_w},\quad \bar k_w = \tfrac{\sum_c n_c k_c}{\sum_c n_c}.
  \]
\end{itemize}

Each attribute is min--max normalised within the pool; utility $U_i$ is identity for $a_1$--$a_5$ and mildly concave on $a_6$.

\textbf{Four donor-preference profiles} represent plausible stakeholder weightings (Table~\ref{tab:profiles}).

\textbf{Evidence-bounded gap score.} For crisis $u$ under profile $p$,
\begin{equation}
G_p(u) \;=\; \sum_{i \in \mathcal{O}(u)} w_{p,i}\,U_i\!\bigl(\tilde a_i(u)\bigr),
\label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{O}(u)$ is the set of signals actually observed. We sum rather than renormalise by $\sum_{\mathcal{O}} w$: a country's maximum possible score is bounded by its observed weight mass, so incomplete data cannot inflate rank. The cost~--- systematic under-ranking of poorly-documented crises~--- is declared via the completeness flag $c(u) = |\mathcal{O}(u)|/6$ attached to every score. The alternative (renormalising) collapses the CERF-UFE top-5 overlap from 100\,\% to 0\,\% on our pool; see \S\ref{sec:val}.

\textbf{Disagreement band and four-cell typology.} The four profile-specific ranks yield a median rank $\widetilde{\text{rank}}(u)$ and an IQR across profiles. A median split on IQR against a median split on $a_6$ produces the four-cell typology (Table~\ref{tab:typology}).

\midrule
\textbf{Sectors uneven} & consensus sector-starved & contested sector-starved \\

\caption{Four-cell typology from rank-IQR $\times$ cluster Gini.\label{tab:typology}}

\textbf{Temporal decomposition.} Chronic overlookedness is a multi-year rolling mean of the gap; acute is the deviation of the current year from that mean. Both are registered as Level-4 properties (\S\ref{sec:semlayer}).

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Typed Semantic Layer}\label{sec:semlayer}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Every analytic quantity is a named property registered in a single specification file. Seventy-one properties at five levels: \textbf{L1 Observations} (22 raw primitives), \textbf{L2 Ratios} (18 scale-normalised signals), \textbf{L3 Inequality} (10 distribution indices: HHI, Gini, concentration measures), \textbf{L4 Trends} (12 windowed aggregates: baselines, deltas, trend slopes, persistence), \textbf{L5 Composites} (9: four profile-specific gap scores, balanced, median rank, rank-IQR, INFORM composite, completeness). Each property declares its formula, source dataset, input lineage, unit, granularity, and known failure modes. No analytic column exists in code without a registered declaration; a registry class reads the specification at import time so every displayed value carries its provenance. Eight lenses group subsets of the 71 properties by analytic question; six view modes render each lens (ranked atlas, PCA, clustering, per-country profile, cross-lens radar, validation). All 48 lens-by-mode cells render without per-cell code.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limits}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Stated in full, as a first-class part of the method.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Incompleteness is accepted, not imputed.} The completeness flag is attached to every score; partly-documented countries are scored alongside full-data ones under evidence-bounded scoring, and the trade-off is systematic under-ranking rather than inflation.
  \item \textbf{Cluster taxonomy drifts} between HNO and FTS. A simple normalisation (lower-case + non-alphanumeric $\to$ underscore) is used; a curated cluster map is the next step.
  \item \textbf{INFORM methodology rescaling}, February 2026, makes severity indices pre- and post-boundary not comparable. The ontology declares this as a failure mode; temporal aggregates that span the boundary use the stable ordinal category, not the continuous severity score.
  \item \textbf{Small sample.} The pool of country-years with the full six-attribute vector is on the order of tens. Statistical claims are made only against held-out external benchmarks.
  \item \textbf{Two significant figures.} A score of 0.62 is not meaningfully distinct from 0.59; median ranks are always reported with the IQR.
  \item \textbf{Not automation.} The system ranks; humans decide. Every displayed value carries its provenance.
\end{itemize}

\section{Planned Next Step: A Learned Layer}\label{sec:next}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The typed semantic layer is a feature store. The next step is a Level-6 family of \emph{learned} composites above it, registered in the specification alongside the handcrafted ones. Three candidate tasks, ranked by experimental cleanliness:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.3em,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Structural recoverability.} Predict $G_p(u)$ from L1--L3 alone. Reproducible label; tests whether the MAUT formulation is learnable from observations. \emph{Cleanest experiment.}
  \item \textbf{Typology classification.} Four-class target from L1--L3. Same data, discrete label, easier error analysis.
  \item \textbf{Learning external curation.} Binary classification against CERF UFE membership. Most operationally interesting but noisiest, because the label is itself a political judgement.
\end{enumerate}
Model family is constrained to regularised linear and gradient-boosted trees (small pool, interpretability-first). Temporal cross-validation (train $\leq 2024$, test $= 2025$) and country-holdout are both used. The learned layer is never a replacement for the handcrafted scoring in the default view~--- both live side by side, with the learned model exposed as one of the lenses. A predicted ranking of overlookedness can, if communicated carelessly, reinforce existing patterns of neglect with an algorithmic gloss; the learned layer therefore ships with the same completeness-flag discipline as the rest of the ontology and a written use-and-limits note on any export.

\section{Conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

When almost every active response is underfunded, a single coverage ratio stops discriminating. Recasting overlookedness as a joint distribution~--- across donor-preference profiles and across sector inequality within a crisis~--- surfaces the three kinds of overlooked crisis that scalar methods conflate, without requiring data the sector does not already publish. Implemented over a typed semantic layer so every displayed value traces to source data. Evidence-bounded so incomplete data cannot inflate rank. Eighty per cent top-5 overlap with OCHA's CERF UFE on the widest pool, 100\,\% on strict. What remains to be built is a learned layer above the ontology, held to the same interpretability and provenance discipline.
