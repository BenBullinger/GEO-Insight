\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Humanitarian coordinators and donor advisors need rapid, defensible answers to a recurring question: \emph{where does documented need most outpace available funding?} The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks \$33B for 135M people, yet the 2025 appeal raised only \$12B --- the lowest in a decade. Nineteen country contexts are now severely underfunded, up from eight in 2021. GEO-Insight proposes a multi-attribute decision-support system that ranks active crises by the mismatch between need (from HNO/HRP) and coverage (from FTS and CBPF), producing an auditable gap score with an attribute-level explanation per ranked crisis. The scoring framework is adapted from multi-attribute decision-making (MADM) literature and extended with a temporal decomposition to distinguish chronic (structural) neglect from acute (recent) deterioration. Validation targets OCHA's own CERF Underfunded Emergencies list as a ground-truth benchmark. The deliverable is a decision-support prototype --- not an automated allocation engine --- with explicit surfacing of data quality, missingness, and weight sensitivity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

The humanitarian system in 2026 is operating under unprecedented fiscal strain. According to OCHA's Global Humanitarian Overview 2026,\footnote{\url{https://www.unocha.org/publications/report/world/global-humanitarian-overview-2026-enesfr}} agencies are asking \$33B to reach 135 million people, with a hyper-prioritised \$23B floor targeting 87 million lives. The 2025 appeal raised only \$12B, and headline coverage ratios are collapsing: DRC at $\sim$22\%, Yemen and Somalia at $\sim$24\%, Sudan dropping from 69\% in 2024 to 35\% in 2025.\footnote{\url{https://www.thenewhumanitarian.org/news/2025/12/08/five-takeaways-uns-aid-plans-2026}} OCHA now flags 19 contexts as severely underfunded (below 50\% coverage), up from eight in 2021 --- severe underfunding has become the norm rather than the exception.

When \emph{everything} is underfunded, the relevant question shifts from ``which crises are funded?'' to ``relative to documented need, which crises are most overlooked?'' This is precisely the analytical question GEO-Insight addresses. The output is intended for decision support --- informing donor advisors, humanitarian coordinators, and CERF allocation committees --- not for automating allocation decisions, which remain necessarily political and contextual.

\section{Problem Definition}

Given a query or geographic scope (country, region, sector, or natural-language prompt), GEO-Insight returns a ranked list of crises ordered by a \emph{gap score} $G(u) \in [0,1]$, where larger values indicate a larger mismatch between documented need and available coverage. For each ranked entry the system also returns:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item the coverage ratio and per-capita funding shortfall in absolute units;
    \item an attribute-level decomposition of the score, indicating which signals are dominant;
    \item data-quality flags (stale HNO, incomplete funding data, no active HRP);
    \item a brief natural-language explanation suitable for inclusion in a briefing note.
\end{itemize}

The system must handle ambiguous queries gracefully, must not fabricate figures absent from the source data, and must surface uncertainty rather than hide it behind false precision.

\section{Related Work}

We draw methodology primarily from three streams in the disaster operations and decision-support literature.

\paragraph{Multi-attribute decision-making for humanitarian allocation.}
Rye and Aktas~\cite{rye2022} propose EDIS, a decision-support framework for humanitarian cluster resource allocation in the first 72 hours after disaster onset, when real-time assessments are unavailable. Their method combines multi-attribute decision making (MADM), the Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) for preference elicitation, and multi-attribute utility theory (MAUT) for aggregation. The architecture is transferable to our setting: need (people in need, severity) and coverage (funding ratios, per-capita allocations) are the attributes; AHP elicits transparent weights; MAUT composes them into an explainable aggregate. The explicit, auditable weight vector directly addresses the ``defensibility'' requirement of the challenge brief.

\paragraph{AI-DSS failure modes in humanitarian logistics.}
Abdulrashid et al.~\cite{abdulrashid2026}, in a 2026 systematic review of AI-driven decision support for disaster management, catalogue the dominant failure modes: fragmented and incomplete datasets, limited explainability of AI models, siloed data pipelines, and equity concerns in resource distribution. The review concludes that the field needs ``interoperable, interpretable, and context-sensitive AI-DSS frameworks.'' We treat these as design constraints rather than aspirations.

\paragraph{Hybrid DSS and trade-off surfacing.}
Sahebjamnia et al.~\cite{sahebjamnia2017} design a hybrid DSS for humanitarian relief chains combining a stochastic simulator, a rule-based inference engine, and a knowledge base. Their key methodological contribution for our purposes is the \emph{trade-off framing}: they do not collapse multiple performance measures (coverage, cost, response time) into a single scalar but instead surface the trade-off frontier. We adopt the same design principle --- our ranking is a default view, but the system exposes a frontier of coverage-gap vs. need-magnitude vs. per-capita shortfall.

\paragraph{Robustness under uncertainty.}
Vargas Florez et al.~\cite{vargasflorez2015} model humanitarian facility location as a stochastic multi-scenario programme, optimising for resilience when some infrastructure may fail. We do not replicate their optimisation, but we borrow the framing: a crisis consistently underfunded across scenarios (years) should rank differently from one volatile but occasionally well-resourced. This directly motivates the chronic/acute decomposition in Section~\ref{sec:bonus}.

\paragraph{Embedded analytics.}
Griffith et al.~\cite{griffith2017} argue that during time-constrained humanitarian operations, simple and interpretable analytics (decision trees, basic descriptive statistics) operationalised in accessible open-source tools outperform complex black-box methods. They also emphasise that \emph{data integration is the practical bottleneck}, not algorithm choice. We align with this philosophy: the scoring engine is deliberately transparent, and we budget engineering time accordingly, with data ingestion and harmonisation as the primary risk.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Unit of analysis}

The primary unit of analysis is the \emph{country-level crisis response} for an active HRP cycle. A secondary, optional unit is \emph{country $\times$ sector} (e.g., Sudan $\times$ Health, Sudan $\times$ WASH), which addresses the failure mode called out in the challenge brief: a crisis that is well-funded in aggregate but has starved sector clusters. Sector-level analysis is conditional on HNO sector breakdowns being available and recent.

\subsection{Eligibility filtering}

A crisis $u$ enters the ranking pool only if it satisfies all of:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item An active HRP \emph{or} documented PIN above a magnitude threshold $\tau_{\text{PIN}}$ (default $10^6$).
    \item HNO figures not older than $\tau_{\text{stale}}$ months (default 24; configurable).
    \item At least one funding record in FTS for the current or prior plan year.
\end{enumerate}
Crises failing criterion~(1) are still reported in a secondary ``possible blind-spot'' list, because the absence of a formal HRP is itself a signal of institutional overlooking --- but they are not ranked against HRP-backed crises, which would be a category error.

\subsection{Attributes}

For each eligible crisis $u$ we compute five attributes. Let $P(u)$ denote people in need, $R(u)$ funding requirements, $F(u)$ funding received, $N(u)$ country population, and $D(u)$ the vector of per-donor contributions to $u$.

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
a_1(u) &= 1 - \min\!\left(1,\; F(u)/R(u)\right) && \text{coverage shortfall}\\
a_2(u) &= \max\!\left(0,\; R(u)-F(u)\right) / P(u) && \text{per-PIN funding gap (USD)}\\
a_3(u) &= P(u) / N(u) && \text{need intensity (PIN as share of population)}\\
a_4(u) &= \text{severity}(u) \in [1,5] && \text{HNO severity / IPC phase}\\
a_5(u) &= \text{HHI}\!\left(D(u)\right) && \text{donor concentration, Herfindahl index}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Each attribute is oriented so that larger values indicate ``more overlooked.'' Attribute $a_5$ encodes fragility: a crisis funded by one or two donors is one policy change away from collapse even at nominally adequate coverage today.

\subsection{Normalisation and utility}

Each attribute is min-max normalised over the eligible pool to $\tilde{a}_i(u) \in [0,1]$. A per-attribute utility function $U_i:[0,1]\to[0,1]$ is then applied. The default is linear, $U_i(x)=x$; for coverage shortfall ($a_1$) we use a concave utility
\[
U_1(x) = 1 - (1-x)^\gamma, \quad \gamma \in [1.5,\,2.5]
\]
which amplifies the signal in the low-coverage tail (a crisis moving from 10\% to 5\% coverage is worse than one moving from 60\% to 55\%). The concavity parameter is exposed to the user.

\subsection{Weight elicitation (AHP)}

The weight vector $w \in \Delta^4$ is obtained either from (i) a default expert-calibrated profile or (ii) pairwise AHP elicitation from the user. Consistency of pairwise comparisons is checked via the consistency ratio CR; if $\text{CR} > 0.1$ the user is prompted to revise. Three default profiles are shipped:

\subsection{Composite gap score (MAUT additive form)}

\begin{equation}
G(u) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{5} w_i \cdot U_i\!\left(\tilde{a}_i(u)\right)
\label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}

This aggregation follows Rye and Aktas~\cite{rye2022}. The additive form is justified under mutual utility independence of attributes, which we do not strictly verify; we instead note the assumption transparently and offer a multiplicative alternative (geometric mean) as a sensitivity check.

\subsection{Explanation layer}

The per-attribute contribution $c_i(u) = w_i \cdot U_i(\tilde{a}_i(u))$ decomposes the final score. For each top-$K$ crisis we report:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item the two largest $c_i(u)$ values, expressed in plain language (``ranked high because coverage is 18\% \emph{and} per-PIN gap is in the 95th percentile'');
    \item absolute reference figures (PIN, requirements, received, coverage \%);
    \item data-quality caveats (``HNO figures dated 2023; treat as lower bound'').
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Query layer}

Natural-language queries are parsed into a structured filter over the scoring schema via a lightweight LLM call with a fixed prompt and a JSON schema. Example: \emph{``acute food insecurity hotspots with below 10\% funding''} $\Rightarrow$ \texttt{\{sector: "food", severity: $\geq 4$, coverage\_max: 0.10\}}. The LLM is not involved in scoring itself. If the LLM output fails schema validation, the system falls back to keyword-based filtering and flags the degradation in the response.

\section{Structural vs. Acute Neglect (Bonus Task)}\label{sec:bonus}

The challenge brief asks how ranking should differ between crises chronically overlooked for years and those newly deteriorating. We decompose the gap score temporally.

Let $G_t(u)$ be the gap score computed using data available at year $t$. Define:
\begin{align}
\bar{G}_k(u) &= \frac{1}{k}\sum_{t=T-k+1}^{T} G_t(u) && \text{chronic (rolling $k$-year mean)}\\
\Delta G(u) &= G_T(u) - \frac{1}{k-1}\sum_{t=T-k+1}^{T-1} G_t(u) && \text{acute (recent deviation)}
\end{align}

We present these as a 2-D view (chronic axis, acute axis) rather than a single scalar, following the trade-off surfacing principle from Sahebjamnia et al.~\cite{sahebjamnia2017}. Four quadrants emerge with distinct policy implications:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Chronic + stable} (high $\bar{G}$, low $\Delta G$): persistent structural neglect, candidate for multi-year pooled funding.
    \item \textbf{Chronic + deteriorating} (high $\bar{G}$, high $\Delta G$): compounding emergency, highest-priority intervention.
    \item \textbf{Newly underfunded} (low $\bar{G}$, high $\Delta G$): acute shock, candidate for CERF rapid-response window.
    \item \textbf{Stable adequate} (low on both): background pool, monitored but not flagged.
\end{itemize}

For a single scalar when required (e.g., a ranked export), we use $G^\star(u) = \alpha\, \bar{G}_k(u) + (1-\alpha)\max(0,\Delta G(u))$ with $\alpha = 0.6$ by default, favouring the chronic signal.

\section{System Architecture}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Ingestion} --- HDX HAPI client (preferred) with fallback to direct CSV pulls. Pandas dataframes cached locally with dataset version and fetch timestamp.
    \item \textbf{Harmonisation} --- country ISO-3 canonicalisation, sector taxonomy mapping (HNO clusters $\leftrightarrow$ FTS sector codes), plan-year alignment.
    \item \textbf{Scoring engine} --- pure-Python computation of Equations (1)--(2); deterministic, fully unit-testable.
    \item \textbf{Query layer} --- Pydantic schema + LLM-assisted parser for natural-language queries, fallback to keyword routing.
    \item \textbf{Presentation} --- Jupyter notebook for development; Streamlit dashboard for the demo; JSON export for programmatic consumers.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Validation Strategy}

We validate against OCHA's own list of overlooked crises --- the CERF Underfunded Emergencies window --- which allocates roughly one-third of CERF funds twice yearly to ``protracted or neglected humanitarian crises where funding is low but vulnerability and risk levels are high.''\footnote{\url{https://cerf.un.org/apply-for-a-grant/underfunded-emergencies}} This gives us an independent, authoritative, human-curated benchmark.

Metrics:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Overlap@K}: fraction of our top-$K$ ranking that appears on the contemporaneous CERF UFE list.
    \item \textbf{Spearman $\rho$}: rank correlation between our score and CERF inclusion order (where available).
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity band}: how much does the top-$K$ set shift as weights vary across the three default profiles? A low-sensitivity top-$K$ indicates robustness.
    \item \textbf{Stress tests}: deliberate removal of sectors, years, or donor data from inputs to confirm graceful degradation and appropriate data-quality flagging.
\end{itemize}

A strong submission will show that the ranking agrees substantially with CERF UFE for the obvious cases (DRC, Sudan, Yemen) while producing defensible, explainable differences elsewhere.

\section{Handling Uncertainty and Data Quality}

Following Abdulrashid et al.~\cite{abdulrashid2026}, we treat data-quality transparency as a first-class output.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Stale HNO figures.} Each country's HNO vintage is carried through scoring; rankings include an ``evidence age'' field. Where PIN is more than 24 months old the crisis is flagged amber.
    \item \textbf{Funding definition.} We commit to \emph{funding received} as the denominator of coverage, not pledged or requested, and report all three in the per-crisis detail view.
    \item \textbf{Missing sector data.} Sector-level scoring degrades gracefully to country level rather than imputing.
    \item \textbf{Plan-less active crises.} Reported in a separate ``possible blind-spot'' list (Section~5.2) rather than merged into the main ranking.
    \item \textbf{Donor concentration with sparse donor count.} HHI is undefined below two donors; we report ``single-donor risk'' as a categorical flag rather than a numeric score.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}

\paragraph{Decision support, not automation.} The brief is explicit: good humanitarian data work ``respects context.'' GEO-Insight produces rankings, not allocations. Any downstream user should treat a gap score as a hypothesis to investigate, not a verdict.

\paragraph{Visibility bias.} Datasets overrepresent crises with functioning information architectures. A country with a collapsed statistical system (e.g., due to active conflict) may appear lower-ranked purely because its HNO figures are missing or stale. The system surfaces this via evidence-age flags but cannot fully correct for it.

\paragraph{Political sensitivity.} Declaring a crisis ``overlooked'' is a claim with political implications. The system avoids editorial framing in outputs; explanations report numeric facts and attribute contributions, not donor-shaming narratives.

\paragraph{False precision.} Gap scores are reported with at most two significant figures. Confidence intervals from weight-profile sensitivity are always shown alongside point values. Outputs avoid implying that a score of 0.84 is meaningfully distinct from 0.82.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} All data fetches are timestamped and versioned. External LLM calls are logged with prompts and responses. Random seeds (if any) are fixed.
