\small
\textbf{Scope.} This is a student proof-of-concept produced over a hackathon
weekend. It is not a validated operational tool, not peer-reviewed, and not
endorsed by any of the data providers referenced. Its rankings are statistical
estimates with calibrated uncertainty, and should not be used to allocate
humanitarian funding or to substitute for expert judgement. The purpose of the
document is to describe what was built, how it was validated, and where it
might be useful as a starting point for further work.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In 2026, humanitarian agencies asked \$33\,B to reach 135\,M people; the 2025
appeal raised the lowest amount in a decade, with nineteen country contexts
below 50\,\% coverage. When nearly every response is underfunded, a single
coverage ratio stops discriminating. We treat overlookedness as a latent
scalar $\theta$ per country and infer its posterior from six observed
attributes through a hierarchical Bayesian observation model. Attributes are
linked to $\theta$ through likelihoods matched to their natural supports;
slopes are sign-constrained by domain knowledge; partial-data countries are
pooled toward the global mean through a population-level prior. Stakeholder
preferences are encoded as priors over slopes rather than weights in a sum,
so consensus and contested crises are read from posterior overlap. Inference
is variational with NUTS calibration on every fit. The candidate pool is the
22 countries with an active humanitarian response plan. Against two
independently-curated human benchmarks (CERF UFE and CARE \emph{Breaking the
Silence}), the model ties an additive baseline on the two largest benchmark
windows and beats it on the smaller windows; a 2024-only fit predicts the
March-2025 CERF selection at 4/10 precision @ 10 without seeing 2025 data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Context}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The 2026 Global Humanitarian Overview asks \$33\,B for 135\,M people in
need.\footnote{\url{https://www.unocha.org/publications/report/world/global-humanitarian-overview-2026-enesfr}}
The 2025 appeal raised \$12\,B, the lowest in a decade. Coverage ratios have
collapsed: DRC $\sim$22\,\%, Yemen and Somalia $\sim$24\,\%, Sudan from 69\,\%
(2024) to 35\,\% (2025). Nineteen contexts now sit below 50\,\% coverage, up
from eight in 2021.

A single coverage ratio cannot discriminate between these contexts. The useful
variation is no longer \emph{whether} a crisis is underfunded but \emph{how},
and \emph{for whom}. Three kinds of overlookedness become distinguishable once
scoring moves from a scalar to a joint distribution over multiple signals:
\emph{forgotten} (low funding, low attention), \emph{contested} (high attention,
persistent decline), and \emph{sector-starved} (adequate aggregate, one cluster
at 20\,\%). Each warrants a different response, and current scalar tools
conflate them.

Prior work on humanitarian resource allocation using multi-attribute decision
methods (e.g.\ Rye \& Aktas~\cite{rye2022}) shows that rank orderings depend
strongly on the weights chosen by decision-makers. We take that finding as
motivation to model the underlying construct as latent, and to encode
stakeholder preferences as priors on how each signal should inform that
latent, rather than as weights that determine a score directly.

\section{What was built}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Six inputs, one latent, four stakeholders, two external validation sources,
and a typed semantic layer underneath them all.

\textbf{Inputs.} Five datasets from UN OCHA (HNO, HRP, FTS, CoD-PS, CBPF) and
one from the EU Joint Research Centre (INFORM Severity, 67 monthly snapshots
consolidated into long format). All inputs are re-downloaded from source
on first run; no pre-baked caches are shipped. Two externally-curated lists
are used only for validation and never for fitting: CERF Underfunded
Emergencies selections and CARE \emph{Breaking the Silence} annual rankings.

\textbf{Latent.} One unobserved scalar $\theta$ per country. Higher $\theta$
means more overlooked. $\theta$ is never observed; it is inferred jointly
from the six inputs through the observation model in \S\ref{sec:method}.

\textbf{Semantic layer.} Seventy-one analytic properties are registered in
a single specification file, across five derivation levels (L1 raw
observations, L2 scale-normalised ratios, L3 inequality indices, L4 windowed
trends, L5 the Bayesian posterior). Each property declares its formula,
inputs, unit, granularity, and known failure modes. No analytic column
exists in code without a declaration.

\textbf{Surfaces.} The result is exposed through a landing page, a
methodology walkthrough, a slide deck, and two Streamlit applications
(raw-data audit and semantic analysis). Every displayed value traces back
to a registered property and, through it, to a source file.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textbf{Unit of analysis} is country-year. A country is \emph{eligible} if it
has an observed gap per person in need~($a_2$) --- operationally, an active
humanitarian response plan with both HNO and FTS entries. In the 2025 cycle
this is 22 countries. This matches the population CERF UFE selections are
drawn from, so external validation is on the same pool. Countries without
an active response plan return no posterior: the construct ``overlooked
humanitarian crisis'' is not well-defined for them.

\textbf{Six-attribute vector} per eligible country:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
  \item $a_1$ coverage shortfall $= 1 - \min(\text{fund}/\text{req},\,1) \in [0,1]$
  \item $a_2$ gap per PIN $= \max(0,\,\text{req}-\text{fund})/\text{PIN} \in [0,\infty)$
  \item $a_3$ need intensity $= \text{PIN}/\text{population} \in [0,1]$
  \item $a_4$ severity: INFORM 1--5 ordinal
  \item $a_5$ donor concentration (Herfindahl over FTS single-destination incoming flows) $\in [0,1]$
  \item $a_6$ intra-crisis equity: PIN-weighted Gini over sector coverage ratios $\in [0,1]$,
  \[
  a_6(u) = \frac{\sum_{i,j} n_i\,n_j\,|k_i - k_j|}{2\,(\sum_c n_c)^2\,\bar k_w},\qquad
  \bar k_w = \frac{\sum_c n_c\,k_c}{\sum_c n_c}.
  \]
\end{itemize}

Attributes are left on their natural supports; no min--max normalisation is
applied. The observation likelihoods operate on raw scales.

\textbf{Observation model.} For each attribute $i$, a likelihood matched to
its support:
\begin{equation}
a_i(u) \mid \theta(u) \;\sim\;
\begin{cases}
\text{Beta}\bigl(\sigma(\alpha_i + \beta_i\theta)\,\phi_i,\;
                  (1 - \sigma(\alpha_i + \beta_i\theta))\,\phi_i\bigr)
  & i \in \{1, 3, 5, 6\} \\[0.25em]
\text{LogNormal}(\alpha_2 + \beta_2\theta,\; \sigma_2^2)
  & i = 2 \\[0.25em]
\text{OrderedLogistic}(\alpha_4 + \beta_4\theta,\; \kappa)
  & i = 4
\end{cases}
\label{eq:obs}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the logistic function, $\phi_i > 0$ is a per-attribute
precision, $\sigma_2 > 0$ is the log-normal scale, and
$\kappa = (\kappa_1, \ldots, \kappa_4)$ are the ordered-logistic cut-points.
Beta regression respects the bounded support of the four fractional attributes;
the log-normal handles the positive per-PIN gap; the ordered logistic handles
the qualitative spacing of the INFORM severity steps that an interval-scale
treatment would silently distort.

\textbf{Sign constraints.} Every slope is constrained $\beta_i > 0$ via a
half-normal prior. This is the operational definition of $\theta$: the latent
moves with each attribute the way domain knowledge says it should --- higher
coverage shortfall, higher per-PIN gap, more concentrated donors, sharper
sector imbalance, greater need intensity, and higher severity all correspond
to more overlooked. Without sign constraints the model is only weakly
identified and the inferred $\theta$ picks up arbitrary linear combinations.
The location ambiguity ($\theta \mapsto \theta + c$) is resolved by the
population-level prior below.

\textbf{Hierarchical prior on $\theta$.}
\begin{equation}
\mu_\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,0.3),\qquad
\sigma_\theta \sim \text{Half-}\mathcal{N}(0.5),\qquad
\theta(u) \mid \mu_\theta, \sigma_\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\theta,\,\sigma_\theta^2).
\label{eq:hier}
\end{equation}
Countries with sparse data are pulled toward the population mean by the prior;
countries with dense data are barely affected. Sparse-data countries get wider
posteriors by construction, not because we down-weight them manually.

\textbf{Stakeholders as priors over slopes.} A stakeholder $s$ is encoded as
a prior on the six slopes,
\begin{equation}
\beta_i^{(s)} \sim \text{Half-}\mathcal{N}(m_i^{(s)},\; v_i^{(s)}),\qquad i = 1, \ldots, 6,
\label{eq:stake}
\end{equation}
producing a separate posterior $p(\theta \mid D, s)$ per stakeholder. Four
stakeholder priors are pre-registered (CERF, ECHO, USAID, NGO consortia);
the weights used are plausible defaults rather than calibrated from allocation
history (\S\ref{sec:next}). Consensus and contested crises are read from
posterior overlap across stakeholders.

\textbf{Inference.} Variational, with NUTS calibration on every fit. The
production guide is a full-covariance Gaussian; a mean-field guide
underestimates posterior variance by ${\sim}3\times$ on this model
(Spearman $\rho = 0.68$ against NUTS), whereas the full-covariance guide
captures the latent-slope correlations and recovers $\rho = 0.89$. Adam,
learning rate $3\times 10^{-3}$, 8{,}000 SVI steps; full run ${\sim}3$\,s
on CPU. NUTS reference: 1{,}000 warmup $+$ 2{,}000 samples $\times$ 4 chains,
${\sim}24$\,s. Reported credible intervals are SVI estimates; the SVI-vs-NUTS
calibration is reported alongside every headline result.

\textbf{Outputs.} Per country: posterior median $\widehat\theta(u)$, 5th and
95th posterior percentiles forming a 90\,\% credible interval, and CI width.
Rankings come from the posterior median; pairs of countries with overlapping
CIs are flagged as not differentiable from the data alone. A median split on
CI width against a median split on sector equity ($a_6$) produces the
four-cell typology in Table~\ref{tab:typology}.

\section{Validation}\label{sec:val}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Two human-curated external benchmarks, neither used in fitting. CERF UFE
(the UN Emergency Relief Coordinator's twice-yearly allocations to underfunded
crises) indexes an underfunding-and-consensus axis. CARE \emph{Breaking the
Silence} (annual top-10 by media attention) indexes an under-reporting axis.
Both are produced by methodology independent of anything in this paper.

As an ablation, the Bayesian posterior is compared against a \emph{naïve
mean} --- the unweighted mean of the six standardised attributes, computed
over only the attributes observed for each country. This is the simplest
plausible scorer; no latent variable, no uncertainty, no stakeholder priors.
The Bayesian result is the proposal; the naïve mean is the floor we aim to
clear.

\textbf{Calibration.} The variational posterior is validated against NUTS
on every fit. The full-covariance Gaussian guide runs in ${\sim}3$\,s and
recovers NUTS posterior medians at Spearman $\rho = 0.89$.

\textbf{Posterior predictive checks.} For each of the six attributes, 1{,}000
replicates are drawn from the fitted posterior and compared against the
observed marginal. Coverage of the 90\,\% predictive interval is
$\geq 0.91$ on every attribute (target 0.90). Per-country Pearson correlation
between predicted mean and observed value is high for coverage shortfall
(0.76) and donor concentration (0.82) and weak for need intensity, sector
Gini, and severity --- attributes that sit near their ceilings across the
HRP pool (most HRP countries have PIN $\approx$ population and severity
$\in \{4, 5\}$) and carry little cross-country variation for the latent to
recover. The latent is best read as a funding-overlookedness axis.

\textbf{Temporal held-out.} A genuine out-of-sample check: fit on the 2024
cycle only (HRP 2024, FTS through end-2024, INFORM through end-2024), then
score the 2024-fit ranking against CERF UFE selections made in 2025. The
2024 fit predicts CERF's March-2025 selection at 4/10 precision @ 10 ---
without ever seeing 2025 data. Year-over-year top-10 overlap is 7/10: HND,
HTI, MOZ, SLV, SOM, TCD, VEN persist; ETH, MLI, SYR drop out (their
2024-cycle severity receded); CMR, GTM, NER enter on 2025 evidence.

\section{Where this might be useful}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Treat the model as decision support, not automation. Three ways a posterior
with calibrated uncertainty and explicit stakeholder lenses might be more
useful than a single coverage ratio:

\textbf{1. Ranking overlapping crises honestly.} Across the top-10, 90\,\%
credible intervals overlap substantially. The defensible claim is
\emph{membership} in the set, not ordering within. A user who wants an
ordered list should know that the order is less supported than the set;
the CI-width column makes that explicit.

\textbf{2. Separating consensus from contested.} Five of the 22 HRP-eligible
countries sit in the top-10 under all four stakeholder priors (CERF, ECHO,
USAID, NGO): HND, SLV, GTM, HTI, SOM. Eleven others appear in the top-10
under some priors but not others. The separation is informative: full-
consensus crises are robust to stakeholder preferences; contested crises
surface trade-offs that merit a deliberate decision rather than an averaged
score.

\textbf{3. Explaining why a country ranks where it does.} Because every
attribute is a registered property with declared provenance, the posterior
can be decomposed into attribute contributions. The landing page and the
analysis app both expose this decomposition: for each top-10 country, which
of the six attributes pull $\theta$ up, with what magnitudes.

These are uses for which a small POC tool can be a starting point. Anyone
adopting the approach seriously would need the extensions listed in
\S\ref{sec:next}, independent review, and an operational data pipeline.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limits}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Stated in full.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Incompleteness is structural, not imputed.} Sparse-data
  countries get wider posteriors because their likelihood has fewer terms;
  the population-level prior pools them toward the global mean. No attribute
  is ever filled in.
  \item \textbf{The candidate pool is HRP-eligible only ($n = 22$).} Countries
  without an active humanitarian response plan return no posterior. This is
  a deliberate scientific choice. Empirically, expansions of the gate (to
  countries with positive FTS requirements but no HRP, or with $\geq 4/6$
  attributes observed) degraded external precision @ $k$.
  \item \textbf{Stakeholder priors are uncalibrated.} The four pre-registered
  priors use plausible defaults rather than weights learned from each agency's
  actual allocation history. The consensus/contested readout is therefore a
  methodological framework rather than an empirical claim about these four
  organisations.
  \item \textbf{Variational posteriors may underestimate variance.} A
  mean-field guide under-covers when latent parameters are correlated; the
  full-covariance guide adopted here captures those correlations and is
  calibrated against NUTS on every fit. Reported CI widths remain SVI
  estimates.
  \item \textbf{Sign constraints are domain-knowledge commitments.} All six
  slopes are constrained $\beta_i > 0$. A new attribute that does not
  co-vary positively with overlookedness would need an opposite-sign prior
  or a different observation likelihood.
  \item \textbf{Cluster-taxonomy drift} between HNO and FTS. A simple
  normalisation (lower-case + non-alphanumeric $\to$ underscore) is applied;
  a curated cluster map is the next step.
  \item \textbf{INFORM methodology rescaling} (February 2026) makes severity
  indices pre- and post-boundary not directly comparable. Temporal aggregates
  spanning the boundary use the stable ordinal category, not the continuous
  severity score.
  \item \textbf{Posterior medians without CIs are misleading.} Every reported
  ranking carries the CI as a column.
  \item \textbf{Not automation.} The system ranks; humans decide. Every
  displayed value carries its provenance.
  \item \textbf{External benchmarks are imperfect proxies.} CERF UFE reflects
  a political process on top of the data; CARE BTS reflects media attention.
  Agreement with them is evidence; disagreement is not necessarily a failure.
\end{itemize}

\section{What remains to be done}\label{sec:next}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Three extensions, in order of maturity. Items 1 and 2 are planned;
item 3 is prototyped and reports measured numbers.

\textbf{1. Calibrated stakeholder priors.} The slope priors in
equation~\ref{eq:stake} are pre-registered with default values. Regressing
actual allocation histories on the six attributes would recover \emph{effective}
priors for each agency empirically, turning the consensus/contested typology
from a methodological commitment into an empirical readout.

\textbf{2. Temporal AR(1) on $\theta$.} Cross-sectional fits at distinct
time points (\S\ref{sec:val}) already show stable cross-cycle agreement and
useful held-out precision. The natural next layer is a per-country AR(1):
$\theta(u, t) = \mu_u + \rho_u\bigl(\theta(u, t-1) - \mu_u\bigr) + \eta_u(t)$.
Annual HRP/FTS cycles give too few time points on their own; INFORM severity
is monthly and would anchor a partial-temporal model in which chronic ($\mu_u$)
and acute ($\theta - \mu_u$) components fall out of the same posterior.

\textbf{3. Monthly severity forecast (prototyped).} A Mamba-style selective
state-space model (two blocks, 19\,k parameters) reads 24 months of the
INFORM panel --- severity plus seven sub-indicators on IPC phase, displacement,
access, and fatalities --- and forecasts the mean severity $H$ months ahead
on the 1--5 scale. The encoder sees no funding data, no stakeholder priors,
and no Bayesian posterior; the input is strictly the monthly panel. Temporal
split at January 2025.

Held-out MAE is reported against three baselines at each horizon to avoid
overclaiming:

countries with no net precision gain across the four external benchmarks.
The forecasting result above is the standalone contribution; the integration
into the Bayesian model is extension, not uplift.

\section{Summary}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The project treats overlookedness as a latent scalar and infers its posterior
from six observations through a hierarchical Bayesian model. Every ranking
carries a 90\,\% credible interval. Stakeholder preferences are encoded as
priors over slopes rather than weights in a sum. The variational posterior
is calibrated against NUTS on every fit. Against two independent human-curated
benchmarks the model ties an additive baseline on the larger windows and
beats it on the smaller windows and on the media-attention benchmark; a
2024-only fit predicts the March-2025 CERF selection at 4/10 precision @ 10.
The tool is a student proof-of-concept; anything operational would need the
extensions above, independent review, and an operational data pipeline.
